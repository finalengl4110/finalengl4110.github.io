Definitions

  • Affordance: a possibility available in the environment to an individual independent of the individual’s ability to perceive this possibility.

  • Content management: the process of managing electronic content through its lifecycle from creation, review, storage and dissemination to destruction.

  • eXtensible Markup Language (XML): an open source, standardized, customizable markup language that renders content in any publishing system or by any business process. Since XML is not proprietary software, consumers customize it to suit their business needs when separating content from presentation and properly labeling and storing it in a database to enable reuse and repurposing and delivery of content in dynamic ways to end consumers.

  • Intelligent content: content that can be managed efficiently and dynamically delivered to an unlimited range of targets using high-precision automation. Intelligent content is not limited to one purpose, technology, or output. Intelligent content is consumer focused and consumer driven content.

  • Job crafting: the physical and cognitive changes individuals make in the task or relational boundaries of the work.

  • Meaning of work: individuals’ understanding of the purpose of their work and what they believe they achieve through it.

  • Meaningfullness of work: how much purpose or significance the work has to individuals.

  • Multiple audiences: common core set of single source information with added customized content directed to specific audiences.

  • Multiple information products: information utilizing a common core of single source content but then customized according to the type of presentation output chosen by users such as training materials that are videos or a text knowledge base.

  • Multiple media output: single source content with different presentation.

  • Multiple platforms: single source, customized content relevant to platform presentation.

  • Multiple releases: new versions of products with common core single sourced information sets accompanied with version specific information released in parallel or consecutively.
    • br
  • Product families: product suites that utilize single source content customized to each unique product.

  • Prosocial motivation: the desire to take action that benefits other people or to see the purpose of our work as making a positive difference in others’ lives.

  • Purpose: the understanding that work is linked to mutual objectives.

  • Recognition: another person acknowledging and appreciating the completed work.

  • Single sourcing: producing documents designed to be recombined and reused across projects and various media. It is writing information once and using it many times. Single sourcing is not writing an information product then copying and pasting it into another source or modifying the information for different needs that creates multiple sources. Instead, information elements are "referenced" into the document for reuse or drawn from a database.

  • Work motivation: the force that energizes, directs, channels, maintains, and sustains human behavior.
